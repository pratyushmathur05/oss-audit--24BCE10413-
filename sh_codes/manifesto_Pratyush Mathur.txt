  Every day I rely on github — a tool I did not have to buy,
  did not have to beg permission to use, and can inspect down
  to its last line of code if I choose to. This is not a small
  thing. It is the direct result of people choosing to build
  in the open and share their work freely.

  To me, freedom in software means independence. Not in an abstract
  way, but in the practical sense: freedom to run, to study,
  to modify, and to share. The four freedoms that the Free
  Software Foundation articulates are not idealism. They are
  the conditions under which knowledge compounds rather than
  stagnates. Every open-source tool that exists is evidence
  of what becomes possible when those conditions are met.

  If I could build one thing and share it freely, it would be
  ai medication. I would license it under GPL v2 — not because
  it would make me famous, but because it is the surest way
  to ensure that whatever I build remains available, forever,
  to everyone who might find it useful. Standing on the
  shoulders of giants means, eventually, being someone else's
  giant. This is my commitment to that chain.
